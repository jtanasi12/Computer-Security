Step 1

Primes

In RSA encryption, we use a special number 'n' made by multiplying two big secret numbers, 'p' and 'q'. Now, if I give you 'n', it's like giving you a locked box without the key. Here's the cool part: figuring out 'p' and 'q' from 'n' is like cracking the toughest code ever. This is why RSA encryption is super secure. So, to get started with RSA, just pick your two secret numbers and let the encryption magic begin!

Security: The security of RSA encryption relies on the difficulty of factoring the modulus n into its prime factors p and q. The larger the modulus n (and hence the prime factors p and q), the more computationally difficult it becomes to factorize it, making the encryption more secure.

Public key

The RSA method utilizes a modulus n, also known as the product

n = p × q = 143 (8 bit)

However, for secure communication, much larger primes yielding n values with several thousand binary digits are employed. The public key in RSA comprises the modulus n and an exponent e.

The public key is primarily used for encrypting messages, ensuring that only the intended recipient, who possesses the corresponding private key, can decrypt and read the message. This provides confidentiality for the transmitted data, even if it's intercepted by unauthorized parties.

Private Key

RSA encryption relies on the Euler φ function of n, denoted as φ(n), calculated as (p − 1) × (q − 1), where p and q are distinct prime factors of n. This function ensures security by generating a secret key. For security, φ(n) and the encryption exponent e must be coprime, meaning their gcd is 1.

This ensures no common factors, enhancing encryption security.The secret key d is derived such that e × d ≡ 1 (mod φ(n)), ensuring efficient decryption. d can be efficiently determined, given p, q, and e, typically through algorithms like the extended Euclidean algorithm.

In summary, RSA encryption relies on φ(n) for secure key generation, ensuring mathematical relationships between encryption and decryption keys. The determination of d involves satisfying the mathematical relationship with e and φ(n), achieved using algorithms like the extended Euclidean algorithm.

Step 4

Encryption

In the RSA encryption process, which operates solely on numerical values within the range of 0 to n - 1, a message m (a number) is encrypted using the public key(n, e) as follows:

The encrypted message m'is computed as the result of raising m to the power of e modulo n , expressed as:

m' = m^e (mod n)

Before encryption, the message m may undergo padding to ensure it meets certain requirements, such as being of the appropriate length or providing additional security.

Step 5

Decryption

Decrypting with the private key (n, d) is done analogously with m'' = (m')^d (mod n).

This is m'' = m^(e × d) (mod n).

The decryption process utilizes the private key (n,d), which should be kept confidential and known only to the intended recipient. The private exponent d is derived from e through modular arithmetic and is used exclusively for decrypting messages encrypted with the corresponding public key.

Similar to encryption, the decryption process may involve padding schemes to ensure compatibility and security. Padding is applied to the decrypted message to recover the original message structure and ensure proper decryption
